IMR Tutorial

Overview

The Incident Management Review (IMR) is based on the principle that a resilient incident management function can improve an organization's overall cyber resilience. The IMR consists of a series of questions, the answers to which provide insights into how an organization can improve its ability to identify, analyze, and respond to events and incidents in a repeatable manner. The IMR also enables an organization to assess its event and incident handling capabilities relative to v1.1 of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) with IMR assessment results depicted in terms of the NIST CSF.

An “Incident Management Function” is the part(s) of the organization that provides incident handling and response services for the organization. It is responsible for the performance of all incident handling and response practices. For the sake of simplicity, the term “incident management personnel” refers to the groups or individuals who perform the Incident Management Function activities. Further, the term “organization” refers to the entire [sovereign] group, including the incident management personnel. We use the term “stakeholders” to identify those with a vested interest in the organization's Incident Management Function or in its activities, whether internal or external to the organization.

The IMR Assessment

The IMR Assessment is a lightweight assessment method created by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) for the purpose of evaluating an organization's cybersecurity incident management practices. The IMR Assessment, consisting of 88 questions, is typically delivered in a three to four hour interactive workshop led by facilitators from CISA.

The IMR Assessment reflects an organization's capabilities only at a single point in time—at the time of the assessment. Even though certain aspects and questions in the IMR Assessment are designed to indicate the organization's ability to sustain cybersecurity incident management practices over time, the organization should not rely on the assessment results as a conclusive expression of the organization's incident management capabilities in the future.

The assessment does not consider:

  • activities that are planned or are in the process of implementation.
  • practices that have not been performed for extended periods of time.

Assessment Scope

Scoping the assessment is critical because answers to the IMR questions must be provided in relation to the organization's set of mission critical services and the assets that underpin those services for which the Incident Management Function has responsibility. The organization's Incident Management Function must be able to respond to events and incidents that affect any of the organization's mission critical services. The IMR is organizational in scope where the Cyber Resilience Review (CRR) is focused on a single service. Some larger organizations may have multiple Incident Management functions. For example, a larger corporation may have a centralized Incident Management Function responsible for responding to issues concerning services provided by the corporate headquarters to its business units. The business units may have their own Incident Management Function to respond to issues with the business units' own critical services. The scope would be the set of critical services that the assessed Incident Management Function has responsibility.

A critical service is defined as:

A set of activities an organization carries out in the performance of a duty or in the production of a product that is so critical to the organization's success that its disruption would severely impact continued operations or success in meeting the organization's mission.

Organizations typically have a set of critical services that define their mission. The focus on those critical services for the assessment—rather than assessing the organization as a whole—helps to scope the assessment and tie the results to the organization's mission.

Below are some examples of organizations and their typical critical services:

  • banks and other financial institutions: clearing and settlement, mortgage application processing
  • emergency services providers: processing 911 calls, dispatch
  • electrical power plants: electricity generation, electricity distribution
  • hospitals: clinical services, prescription management
  • government agencies: court case management, benefit management
  • manufacturing companies: machining operations, product engineering and design
  • airports: air traffic control, fuel management

Practice Questions Structure

Each practice question contains a 3-point response scale to evaluate the degree to which the organization has implemented the practice. Each question has three possible answers: “Yes”, “Incomplete”, and “No”:

  • Yes – the organization fully performs the activity specified in the question.
  • Incomplete – the organization partially performs the activity.
  • No – the organization does not perform the activity at all.

Flag for Review: To the right of the of the three-point response scale is the Flag icon which can be used to flag a practice for review. The list of questions flagged for review is provided in the report named Comments and Marked for Review.

Supplemental Guidance: To aid the facilitator, each question is supported by guidance. The guidance should be referred to for background information, possible examples, potential artifacts, and for criteria that defines the requirements for a “Yes” and an “Incomplete” answer. The guidance is displayed by selecting the Guidance Icon containing the letter “G” located below each question. It is very important that assessors refer to the guidance when evaluating the organization's response to a question to ensure that the intent and scope of the question is understood and that the question answer meets the scoring criteria for a “Yes” or an “Incomplete” answer.

Comments: Selecting this icon will display a text box that may be used to make comments regarding the question. For example, a facilitator, for future reference may take a note as to why an answer was marked incomplete vs. yes. Note, these comments are not included in the official report and are meant to be private to the facilitator. These comments can be found in the Comments and Marked for Review report.

Artifacts/Documents: Easily associate, track, and store artifacts, and other supporting documents for each question throughout the assessment.

References: Selecting the References Icon will display the Options for Consideration associated with each question. The Options for Consideration are normally found in the report and are also made available to the facilitator while performing the assessment by using this feature. The options for consideration provide general background information, example artifacts and attributes, outline general guidelines or activities and their associated information sources that can be used to research and improve the organization's event and incident handling practices and the resilience of the incident management service provided to the organization.

Observations: Selecting this icon will pop up an observation details dialog box used to collect additional information about a specific practice question. The type of information that can be collected include an observation title, importance level, resolution date, issue description, the associated impact, suggested recommendations, responsible individual, etc.

Feedback: Used to provide feedback regarding a question to CISA. For example, a user may comment that the question language is too complex or confusing and suggest alternate wording.

After the last domain in the assessment click “Next” to get to the “Other Remarks” screen. This input field can be used to capture information such as general observations or specific information that was learned during the assessment. For example, if an improvement effort is already underway to address specific domain practices, it can be detailed here to make it part of the official report. Since this text is copied verbatim into the report assessors should use discretion in their commentary.

Asset Types

Like the CRR assessment, the IMR considers four categories of assets: People, Information, Technology, and Facilities. In the IMR assessment, any question asking about assets is referring to all the asset types and must be answered considering all the various asset types combined. Unlike the CRR, the IMR assessment does not have questions that ask about each asset type individually. The definition for each asset type is listed below:

  • People - the staff (both internal and external to the organization) such as people that support data centers or otherwise use information and communications technology to operate, monitor and support the service.
  • Information - account information, technology asset configuration files, operational data, customer information and other information necessary for the delivery of a service.
  • Technology – computers (hardware), software, control systems, or other technology including external information systems used by the organization to deliver the service.
  • Facilities – offices buildings, data centers and other physical structures supporting the delivery of the service.
